Reflection on the MATL Program

Entering the MATL program at the University of Southern Mississippi has been one of the most transformative experiences of my professional life. When I began this degree, I was already deeply committed to teaching Spanish in the elementary classroom, but I did not yet have the theoretical grounding, analytical tools, or reflective practices that I now carry with me every day. This program has shaped not only the way I teach, but also the way I understand myself as an educator, as a researcher, and as a lifelong learner.

One of the greatest gifts of the MATL program has been learning to see teaching through the lens of second language acquisition research. Courses such as Applied Linguistics, Teaching Language, and the Practicum helped me connect classroom practices with the cognitive, social, and emotional processes that shape how children learn another language. Concepts like comprehensible input, the affective filter, interactional space, output opportunities, and sociocultural theory moved from abstract readings into living principles that now guide my classroom decisions. I no longer teach on instinct alone; I teach with purpose, grounding each routine, activity, and interaction in what I know about how language develops.

Just as important has been the program's emphasis on reflective practice. Before beginning the MATL journey, I collected ideas, but not always evidence. Now, I view my classroom with a researcher's curiosity. I ask questions, analyze patterns, and try small interventions with intention. Whether it is classroom discourse, student engagement, target-language use, or inclusive pedagogy, I have learned that meaningful change begins with careful observation and a willingness to examine my own assumptions. The action research process, although frustrating at times, has empowered me to take ownership of my professional growth and understand that improvement is an ongoing cycle, not a destination.

Perhaps one of the most meaningful aspects of the MATL program has been the encouragement to bring my own story, cultural heritage, and identity into my teaching. From medieval Iberian storytelling to Latin American music, from inquiry-based learning to family traditions, I learned that my background is not separate from my teaching; it enriches it. This realization permitted me to teach not only with knowledge, but with authenticity, creativity, and heart.

As I look back on this journey, I see a teacher who has grown in confidence, clarity, and intentionality. I now design instruction with a deeper understanding of both linguistic theory and the realities of young learners. I analyze classroom interactions with a sharper eye. I make decisions based on research, reflection, and compassion. And I feel more equipped than ever to support my students in becoming not only language learners but global citizens.

Completing the MATL program has been more than earning a degree; it has been a personal and professional transformation. I am proud of the teacher I have become, grateful for the guidance and inspiration I received along the way, and excited for the continued growth that lies ahead. This program has reaffirmed my belief that teaching is a craft, one that requires curiosity, reflection, and a commitment to learning alongside our students. And it has grounded me in the knowledge that I am part of a broader community of educators who share the same passion for language, culture, and meaningful learning.
