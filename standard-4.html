Standard 4: Content Knowledge

Standard Definition

The teacher understands the central concepts, tools of inquiry, and structures of the discipline(s) he or she teaches and creates learning experiences that make these aspects of the discipline accessible and meaningful for learners to assure mastery of the content.

Artifact 2: Reflection on Prescriptivism & Descriptivism

Artifact: Video Reflection on Prescriptivism and Descriptivism

Rationale

This reflection demonstrates my understanding of linguistic content knowledge, a central component of InTASC Standard 4, which calls for teachers to "understand the central concepts, tools of inquiry, and structures of the discipline" and to make this knowledge accessible and meaningful for students. In the video, I examine the concepts of prescriptivism and descriptivism, both of which are foundational to language instruction but often misunderstood or implicitly adopted without critical awareness.

In the video reflection, I synthesize key linguistic principles by explaining how prescriptive rules emerge from social, historical, and ideological forces, while descriptive rules reflect how speakers naturally use language. Fromkin, Rodman, and Hyams (2018) highlight this distinction as foundational to linguistic science, emphasizing that linguists focus on documenting how people genuinely use language, rather than prescribing correct usage. By engaging with these ideas, I show that I understand language not as a fixed set of rigid norms but as a dynamic, systematic, and evolving human behavior.

In the video, I explain how prescriptivism, while useful for teaching conventions, academic forms, and clarity, can unintentionally invalidate the linguistic backgrounds of learners if applied rigidly. This is especially important in my Spanish classroom, where many students, including heritage speakers, come with rich language repertoires that may not align with standardized textbook forms. My reflection shows how I've learned to recognize these variations not as "errors," but as meaningful linguistic patterns connected to students' lived experiences.

By contrast, descriptivism encourages teachers to observe, analyze, and value how language is actually used by communities. This perspective aligns with contemporary SLA research, such as the work of Gass, Behney, and Plonsky (2020), which highlights how learners develop interlanguage systems influenced by authentic input and communicative needs. Through this lens, I view my role not simply as correcting students but as guiding them in understanding how Spanish functions across cultural contexts, genres, and purposes.

This reflection strengthens my practice because it shows how I apply linguistic theory to real classroom decisions, how I correct, how I scaffold, how I validate diverse language identities, and how I design instruction that balances communicative authenticity with academic expectations. Ultimately, this video demonstrates my ability to translate disciplinary knowledge into developmentally appropriate, culturally responsive, and pedagogically sound language teaching, fully aligning with the expectations of InTASC Standard 4.

References

Fromkin, V., Rodman, R., & Hyams, N. (2018). An Introduction to Language (11th ed.). Cengage.

Gass, S., Behney, J., & Plonsky, L. (2020). Second language acquisition: An introductory course (5th ed.). Routledge.
